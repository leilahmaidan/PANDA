March’s U.S. Jobs Report Indicates Early Signs of Unemployment Rise

March’s jobs report was released on Friday but don’t expect to see an accurate reflection of what the labor market will look like in a post-pandemic America. For now, the report demonstrated a drop in low wage jobs.

March's job report showed the begining stages of the rise in unemployment.

By Laila Hmaidan
Published April 12, 2020 8:00 PM

The Labor of Bureau Statistics’ jobs report was surveyed in the first half of March, just missing the mark before the economy rolled over. Additionally, February’s unemployment rate was still dropping as the economy added 237,000 jobs. A signal that the timing of this month’s report may not only be too early but cushioned by a strong job market that was at a 50-year low.

In contrast, the third week of March saw 3.3 million unemployment claims followed by an additional 6.6 million Thursday morning, all numbers that won’t be included in tomorrow’s report. The consensus forecast had a huge range, from 100k jobs added to 1.25 million lost.

The factor that had the most impact on the downturn of the economy was the stay-at-home announcement, which began to be more heavily implement in mid-March. The phenomena caused a big wave of layoffs in jobs, as non-essential businesses were ordered to close or scale back, and consumers stopped going out and spending. Before the drastic measure was put in place, many businesses were reluctant to let employees go entirely.

“Employment is always a lagging indicator due to human nature because employers don’t want to fire people. You cut their hours instead,” said Dan North, Chief Economist for Euler Hermes.

Although the unemployment rate rose for all levels of wages, businesses that were hit the hardest were retaurants and bars, resulting in higher unemployment rates for lower wage jobs. The income group that was hit the hardest were workers with less than a high school degree.

More accurate indicators to pay attention to this month are Weekly Number of Hours Worked and new job postings, both of which dropped dramatically. Indeed’s trends for job postings for hospitality and tourism plunged by 58% from this time last year. Even postings for jobs that can be done from home, such as software engineers, dropped by 6%.

“People focus on layoffs but the unseen force that drives employment growth down is a lack of hiring,” said Nick Bunker, Economic Research Director for Indeed.

The recently announced Cares Act attempts to mitigate job loss by offering small businesses forgivable loans if certain rules around retaining employees are followed. But parts of the Bill designed to help those that lose their job, such as increased access to unemployment benefits, gives companies a stronger incentive to let workers go, knowing they will be ok, a factor that may be driving unemployment up.

Early indications of job loss could be widespread across most industries but with a big concertation in leisure, entertainment and retail manufacturing. Additionally, indicators that are normally seen as a positive reflection on the economy could signal the opposite under current circumstances. One example is wages, since laid off workers are more likely to be in lower wage growth industries, as they drop off, the average wage rate would go up.

The long-term projection remains open ended as the driving force behind the crash is not economic, but a virus that’s behavior remains widely unknown. When and how the pandemic will end and if it becomes seasonal are all factors that will impact when the economy can start its recovery.

“Public health policy is economic policy and economic policy is health policy,” said Martha Gimbel, Manager of Economic Research for Schmidt Futures.

If the pandemic continues for months and lock-down requests stay in place, the effects could be devastating. Economists’ forecast unemployment ranges from the low double digits to as far-fetched as 30%.

The biggest worry is that when this is all over, businesses won’t want to invest in capital goods and infrastructure, and confidence in the market is lost, all of which would cause a permanent impact on the economy, jobs and a very long road to recovery.
